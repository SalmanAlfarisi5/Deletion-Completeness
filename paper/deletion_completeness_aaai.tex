\section{Problem Formulation}\label{sec:formulation}

\paragraph{Setup.} Let $f$ be a target fact with value $v$ and let $\Sigma(v)$ be
its set of ground-truth surface forms. A memory store $S$ is a set of records; a
deletion operation yields a surviving store $S'\subseteq S$. The adversary
$\mathcal{A}$ has full read access to $S'$, black-box access to a base model $M$,
and general world knowledge $W$; a query $q_f$ asks for $v$. Write
$\mathrm{rec}(a)=1$ iff answer $a$ reveals a surface form in $\Sigma(v)$ (within
the stated value), as scored by the validated recovery judge
(\S\ref{sec:res-judge}). We decompose recoverability into three channels.

\paragraph{Definition 1 (Residual survival).}
$\sigma_{\mathrm{res}}(f,S')=\mathbb{1}\!\left[\exists\, r\in S':\;
\Sigma(v)\cap\mathrm{txt}(r)\neq\emptyset\right]$: a surviving record physically
contains the value.

\paragraph{Definition 2 (Re-derivation).}
$\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}(f,S')=\mathrm{rec}\!\left(\mathcal{A}(q_f \mid
S',M,W)\right)$: the adversary reconstructs $v$ by reasoning over the
\emph{surviving store} plus world knowledge.

\paragraph{Definition 3 (Parametric floor).}
$\rho(f)=\mathrm{rec}\!\left(\mathcal{A}(q_f \mid \emptyset, M, C)\right)$, where
$C\subseteq W$ is the subject's world-knowable context and the store is
\emph{empty}. $\rho$ has no dependence on $S$ and is therefore
\emph{system-independent}; we estimate it as a recovery rate over stochastic
samples.

\paragraph{Definition 4 (Recoverability).}
$R(f,S')=\max\!\left(\sigma_{\mathrm{res}},\,\sigma_{\mathrm{red}},\,\rho\right)$.
A single surviving channel suffices to recover $v$, so the channels combine by
$\max$, not by sum.

\paragraph{Definition 5 (Deletion-completeness; never-stored prior).}
Following the \emph{meaningful-erasure} criterion for deletion under dependencies
\citep{p2e2}, the \emph{never-stored prior} recoverability of $f$ is exactly
$\rho(f)$: what $\mathcal{A}$ recovers had $f$ never been stored. A deletion is
\textbf{complete} iff it returns recoverability to this prior, i.e.,
$\sigma_{\mathrm{res}}=\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}=0$ so that $R(f,S')=\rho$; it is
\textbf{$\tau$-certifiable} iff additionally $R(f,S')<\tau$, equivalently
$\rho<\tau$. This is the never-stored prior ported from the database setting:
completeness is indistinguishability, up to $\tau$, from a world in which $f$ was
never inserted---accounting for the dependencies (other stored facts) that may
re-imply it.

\paragraph{Remark 1 (the limit, C5).} When $\rho\geq\tau$ the fact is
\emph{complete but not certifiable}: $\rho$ carries no store dependence, so no
co-deletion can lower it and $R(f,S')\geq\tau$ irreducibly. The certificate
correctly returns \textsc{incomplete} even at $\sigma_{\mathrm{res}}=
\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}=0$ (e.g.\ R11, Table~\ref{tab:certs}).

\paragraph{Remark 2 (residual--floor boundary, C1/C5).} The same constraining
context can land in either channel depending on \emph{where it lives}. If $v$ is
pinned by a stored operand $o\in S'$, the recovery is re-derivation
($\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}$) and is removable by co-deleting $o$; if $v$ is pinned by
world-knowable context $C\subseteq W$, the recovery is parametric ($\rho$) and is
\emph{not} removable. Our multi-hop operands are synthetic and live only in $S'$,
so their world-knowledge component is generic and inert without the stored
operand: $\rho=0$ and $\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}\!\to\!0$ under co-deletion
(\S\ref{sec:res-rederiv}). Our gradient contexts are themselves world-knowable and
target-constraining, so they enter $W$: $\rho>0$ (\S\ref{sec:res-rho}). This
boundary---deletable re-derivation vs.\ irreducible floor---is the C1/C5 spine.

\paragraph{The optimization problem.} Let $R_f\subseteq S$ be $f$'s own records.
\textsc{Opt-P2E2} seeks a minimal co-deletion set:
\begin{equation*}
\min_{D\,\subseteq\, S\setminus R_f} |D| \quad\text{s.t.}\quad
R\!\left(f,\; S\setminus(R_f\cup D)\right) < \tau ,
\end{equation*}
i.e., close every recovery channel while disturbing as few bystander facts as
possible ($|D|$ is the collateral $k$).

\paragraph{Proposition 1 (NP-hardness).} \textsc{Opt-P2E2} is NP-hard. The
re-derivation paths of $f$ form a hypergraph $H$ whose hyperedges are the minimal
subsets of $S\setminus R_f$ that jointly re-imply $v$; a feasible $D$ must
intersect every hyperedge, so minimizing $|D|$ is \textsc{Minimum Hitting Set},
which is NP-hard, and the hardness of erasure under dependencies is established by
\citet{p2e2}. We therefore adopt the greedy, entailment-confidence-ordered planner
of \S\ref{sec:method}, which is exact on the small dependency graphs that arise in
practice (mean $k=1.17$, \S\ref{sec:res-planner}).

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:results}

We evaluate on three deployed agent-memory systems spanning three architecture
families: \textbf{Mem0} (\texttt{mem0ai} 2.0.7, a deduplicating extraction
pipeline over local Chroma), \textbf{Zep/Graphiti} (\texttt{graphiti-core} 0.29.2,
a bi-temporal knowledge graph over Neo4j~5.26), and \textbf{Letta/MemGPT}
(\texttt{letta}~0.16.8, an LLM-paging agent over Postgres + pgvector). Every
component runs locally with pinned versions and disabled telemetry, so the
pipeline is reproducible from a clean checkout. The deletion adversary has full
read access to the surviving store and black-box access to the base model (no
training-time access). Two pinned reasoners are used wherever recovery is
model-dependent: \texttt{gpt-4o-mini-2024-07-18} (primary) and
\texttt{gpt-4o-2024-08-06}; embeddings are local \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2}. The
completeness threshold is $\tau=0.10$. Datasets are synthetic subjects placed in
realistic statistical contexts: 12 isolated PII facts, 6 multi-hop facts with
their stored operands and bystanders, and 15 parametric-gradient facts across
three difficulty tiers. Recovery is scored by a validated LLM judge with no
observed false accepts (\S\ref{sec:res-judge}: $0/8$ gold negatives, $95\%$
Wilson upper bound $\approx\!0.32$), so under that bound \emph{every reported
recovery rate is a conservative lower bound}. Table~\ref{tab:master} summarizes
the headline numbers; results are presented in decreasing order of confidence.

\subsection{Minimal Co-Deletion: The Planner}\label{sec:res-planner}

Our central operational claim is that completeness is reachable with
\emph{minimal collateral}. On the 6 multi-hop targets, the threshold planner
(probe~$\to$ purge the target's own artifacts~$\to$ co-delete entailing facts in
entailment-confidence order, re-probing until recoverability $<\tau$) achieves
\textbf{100\% completeness with 0 spurious bystander deletions} and a
\textbf{mean collateral of $k=1.17$}; minimality spares 4 operands whose deletion
was unnecessary once a sufficient subset was removed.

\textbf{Control.} The 0-spurious figure \emph{is} the selectivity test: the
candidate pool mixes true operands with bystanders the planner must not touch. The
result is also robust to the entailment judge: re-running with the stronger
\texttt{gpt-4o} judge leaves completeness, spurious count, and $k$ unchanged. This
robustness is not incidental---our entailment-judge validation
(\S\ref{sec:res-judge}) shows \texttt{gpt-4o-mini} false-fires on $41.7\%$ of
\emph{insufficient} partial operands while \texttt{gpt-4o} false-fires on $0\%$,
so using \texttt{gpt-4o} is precisely what prevents $k$ from inflating. The
planner targets the NP-hard Opt-P2E2 problem; ``minimal'' here denotes a
sufficient co-deletion subset, not a certified optimum.

\subsection{The Parametric Floor and the Limit of Certifiable Deletion}\label{sec:res-rho}

Not every fact can be certified deleted. We \emph{measure} the parametric floor
$\rho$---the rate at which the base model alone reconstructs a value from
world-knowable context---rather than asserting it. Across the gradient, $\rho$ is
monotone in tier (\texttt{gpt-4o-mini}\,/\,\texttt{gpt-4o}):
\textbf{low $0.00/0.00$}, \textbf{mid $0.033/0.633$}, \textbf{high $0.833/1.00$}
(Table~\ref{tab:rho}). Consequently \textbf{6 of 15 certificates are
\emph{not} certifiable-complete} ($\rho>\tau$) \emph{even though residual survival
is $0$}. This is the limit result: when the value is recoverable from
non-deletable world knowledge, no amount of co-deletion can certify completeness.
This gradient $\rho>0$ does not contradict the $\rho{=}0$ of the multi-hop facts
in \S\ref{sec:res-rederiv}: there the world-knowledge component is generic and
inert without the (fictional) stored operand, so co-deletion collapses $\rho$ to
$0$; here the context is \emph{itself} world-knowable \emph{and}
target-constraining, so $\rho$ stays positive. This residual--vs--floor boundary
is the C1/C5 spine and is formalized in \S\ref{sec:formulation}.

\textbf{Control.} The $\rho$ probe sees \emph{only} the subject's world-knowable
context, never the stored facts, so co-deletion provably cannot lower it. Three
hardening checks back the gradient: a mid-tier numeric-tolerance sweep shows the
floor persists at every tolerance (its magnitude, not its existence, is
tolerance-dependent); a base-rate vs.\ context-lift split separates genuine
inference from prior plausibility; and a high-tier refusal audit (e.g., the
sensitive-attribute item R13, refused on $5/6$ samples by \texttt{gpt-4o-mini})
means $\rho$ for sensitive attributes is an \emph{adversarial lower bound}. A
\textsc{complete} certificate is complete \emph{modulo} the recovery judge's
$\approx\!78\%$ recall; \textsc{incomplete} and aggregate figures are safe lower
bounds.

\subsection{Decomposing Recovery: Re-Derivation at Zero Residual}\label{sec:res-rederiv}

A fact can be unrecoverable by inspection yet trivially \emph{re-derivable} from
surviving facts. Using an operands-only control---the post-deletion state in
which the target's own value is never stored, so residual survival is $0$ by
construction (verified with the narrow deletion surface form, not the broad
recovery set, so an operand sharing a token is not miscounted)---we isolate the
re-derivation channel. On Mem0, the leak is \textbf{100\% for stored-alone (bin~1)
and $80\%$ for stored+world (bin~2)}---identical across both reasoners
(\texttt{gpt-4o-mini}, \texttt{gpt-4o})---collapsing to \textbf{$0\%$ after
co-deleting the operands}, with parametric floor $\rho=0\%$. The same control
\textbf{ports unchanged to Letta}: bin~1 $100\%$, bin~2 $80\%$
(\texttt{gpt-4o-mini})\,/\,$100\%$ (\texttt{gpt-4o}), again $\to 0\%$ after
co-deletion. Re-derivation is thus a recovery channel orthogonal to residual
survival, and minimal co-deletion closes it down to the parametric floor.

\textbf{Control.} Recovery is binned by mechanism and \emph{never} aggregated
across bins, and run on two reasoners, because re-derivation is model-dependent
(the honest negative F043, a CHS$\to$Bishan geographic leap, does not leak on
either). Crucially, on Letta the co-deletion is issued through the
\emph{direct, deterministic} \texttt{passages.delete} (by captured passage id),
not a conversational request, and we \emph{verify} every operand passage is gone:
faithful co-deletion $=100\%$, bystanders intact $=100\%$. ``Re-derivation
closed'' therefore cannot be confounded with ``the delete silently did not
execute''---keeping this result cleanly separate from the agent-mediated-deletion
finding of \S\ref{sec:res-converge}.

\subsection{Generalization: Three Families, One Phenomenon}\label{sec:res-converge}

Residual survival is not a Mem0 quirk; it recurs across all three families by
\emph{different by-design mechanisms}. The two cross-system probes here are run on
$n{=}3$ sensitive-PII targets per system (a deliberately small, high-stakes set;
see \S\ref{sec:limitations}). In \textbf{Graphiti}, the explicit
\texttt{remove\_episode} is clean for the graph---it hard-deletes the episode,
its \texttt{RELATES\_TO} edges, and orphaned entities (we verify the hard delete
first)---yet the deleted fact survives in \textbf{stale entity and community
summaries that are not recomputed on deletion}: edge residue $33\%$ but
\textbf{summary residue $67\%$}. In \textbf{Letta}, deletion is agent-mediated
across two surfaces (core blocks and archival passages). An explicit dual-surface
instruction is \textbf{$100\%$ faithful}; but a \emph{vague, deployment-realistic}
right-to-be-forgotten request (``I'm not comfortable with you keeping X'') with
the fact also in archival makes the agent scrub the core block it reasons about
and \textbf{silently miss archival}: \textbf{$0\%$ faithful, $100\%$ archival
residue, $0\%$ core residue}. Agent-mediated deletion is \emph{surface-incomplete}
and instruction-dependent.

\textbf{Novelty and corroboration.} This agent-loop failure is genuinely new:
ForgetEval \citep{forgeteval} deliberately bypassed Letta's agent loop
(addressing the archival REST endpoints directly, keeping the LLM out of the
recall hot path), so we test exactly the path it excluded. Conversely,
ForgetEval's own per-system notes \emph{independently corroborate} our convergence
thesis---its observation that Graphiti ``sheds surface forms / synthesised fact
strings'' is our stale-summary residue, and that Mem0's router prefers
``link-and-keep over delete-old'' is our duplication residue.

\textbf{Control.} For Graphiti, verifying the clean hard delete \emph{first}
establishes that the residue is stale summaries, not failed edge removal. For
Letta, the $100\%$-faithful explicit baseline isolates the failure to
phrasing/surface coverage, not to a missing capability.

\subsection{Residual Survival and the Duplication Mechanism}\label{sec:res-residual}

The motivating failure on Mem0 is that naive single-record deletion is residually
incomplete because the store \emph{silently duplicates} facts. Deleting the single
surfaced record leaves the value present in \textbf{$75\%$} of isolated PII
targets; an artifact-aware deletion drives residual survival from
\textbf{$83\%$ to $0\%$}. Both rates are measured over the \emph{same} 12 isolated
PII targets: the $75\%$ ($9/12$) is the stricter duplication-driven criterion (the
deleted value survives because the fact was duplicated), and the $83\%$ ($10/12$)
is the broader naive-arm criterion (any surviving value-bearing row), so the
one-fact gap is an operationalization difference, not a different denominator.

\textbf{Control.} A $2{\times}2$ factorial over embedder
(MiniLM\,/\,OpenAI) $\times$ injection cadence ($0\,$s\,/\,$1.5\,$s) shows
row-inflation of $24$--$42\%$ and duplication incidence of $30$--$39\%$ in
\emph{all four} cells, with paraphrase-variant duplicates outnumbering
byte-identical ones in every cell. The duplication is therefore a semantic-dedup
\emph{design limitation}, not an artifact of our embedder or an injection-timing
race; it is independently documented in Mem0 issues \#4896 (hash-only dedup),
\#4573 ($\sim\!37.6\%$ near-duplicates in a production audit), and \#687 (the
byte-identical write race). We note that these rates are \emph{not} in tension
with ForgetEval's Mem0 numbers: their figure is forget-command success in
top-$k$ retrieval, whereas ours is post-deletion recoverability by \emph{any}
channel---complementary measurements of necessary vs.\ sufficient conditions for
completeness.

\subsection{Judge Validation}\label{sec:res-judge}

Two LLM judges are load-bearing, so both are validated against gold labels. The
\textbf{recovery judge} produced \textbf{no false accepts} on the gold set
($n{=}17$; 7 true positives, 0 false positives, 8 true negatives, 2 false
negatives; $\kappa=0.767$ vs.\ gold). Because this rests on only $8$ negatives,
the $0$ false-accept count carries a $95\%$ Wilson upper bound of $\approx\!0.32$;
the lower-bound framing holds \emph{up to that bound}, and its two errors are both
\emph{conservative} rejections (e.g., scoring ``$\approx$SGD~3{,}142'' as a
non-recovery of $3{,}200$). Tightening this bound with an expanded gold set of
adversarial near-miss \emph{recoveries} (``$\approx$3{,}190'' for $3{,}200$;
``AB+'' for ``AB negative'') is the single most valuable validation extension
(\S\ref{sec:limitations}). The \textbf{entailment judge} is validated on 24 pairs
including 12 hard near-miss negatives (partial operands that should \emph{not}
entail): inter-judge agreement falls from $\kappa=0.83$ on trivial pairs to
$0.455$ on the hard near-misses, and \texttt{gpt-4o-mini} false-fires on $41.7\%$
of insufficient operands versus $0\%$ for \texttt{gpt-4o}. This is the direct
justification for using \texttt{gpt-4o} as the planner's entailment judge
(\S\ref{sec:res-planner}).
