\begin{abstract}
Ask a paging LLM agent to forget a fact and it may scrub the working-memory block
it reasons over while silently leaving the fact in its archival store: the deletion
is acknowledged, looks complete, and is not. More generally, deleting a fact from
an LLM agent's memory does not mean the agent has forgotten it. Existing audits
check whether a forget command removes a value from retrieval, which is
\emph{necessary but not sufficient}, because the value can persist in a derived
artifact or be re-derived from facts that were never deleted. We formalize
\emph{deletion-completeness} for agent memory, decomposing post-deletion
recoverability into three causally distinct channels (residual survival,
re-derivation from surviving facts, and a parametric floor recoverable from the
base model alone) and give a planner that computes the minimal co-deletion
closing the deletable channels, emitting an auditable certificate. Because the
parametric floor cannot be deleted, certification has a hard limit: under the
strongest modeled adversary, \textbf{30 of 81} facts cannot be certified erased
(at threshold $\tau{=}0.1$), even with zero residual survival and no helpful
co-deletion. The same stack, run across three memory architectures (a
deduplication pipeline, a bi-temporal knowledge graph, and a paging agent), finds
residual survival in all three through different by-design mechanisms: the gap is a
property of how agent memory derives and retains facts, not of any single system.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

A user invokes their right to be forgotten \citep{gdpr2016} and asks an LLM agent
to delete their home address. A modern agent with long-term memory (a paging architecture such as
MemGPT/Letta) acknowledges and rewrites the working-memory block it is currently
reasoning over. But the address was also written to the agent's archival vector
store in an earlier session; reasoning only over its working context, the agent
never touches it, and weeks later an ordinary retrieval surfaces the address again.
The deletion was confirmed, looked complete, and was not.

This is not a one-off bug. Even a \emph{faithful} delete of a record need not erase
the fact it carried: the fact can survive in a derived artifact the system built
from it (a duplicate, a summary, a consolidated note) or be re-derived from facts
that remain, such as a salary from a job title plus a public pay band, or a
diagnosis from stored symptoms. The most thorough current audits of agent
forgetting \citep{forgeteval} check whether a forget command removes a value from
top-$k$ retrieval. That check is \emph{necessary but not sufficient}: it does not
ask \emph{why} a deleted fact returns, nor \emph{what minimal further deletion}
would actually close the leak. Those two questions are the subject of this paper.

We cast deletion-completeness as whether a deleted fact remains recoverable, and
decompose recoverability into three causally distinct channels: residual survival
(a surviving artifact still contains the value), re-derivation (surviving facts
entail it), and a parametric floor (the base model recovers it from world knowledge
alone). The first two are closable by deleting more; the third is not. A greedy
planner computes the minimal additional co-deletion that closes the closable
channels, and a certificate records what deletion achieved, at what collateral
cost, and when the parametric floor makes erasure impossible to
certify. Concretely, we contribute:

\begin{itemize}
\item a formal, \emph{adversary-relative} deletion-completeness criterion for agent
memory, strictly stronger than dependency-bounded database erasure, decomposing
recoverability into residual, re-derivation, and parametric channels (\S\ref{sec:formulation});
\item a minimal co-deletion planner for the resulting NP-hard problem, reaching
completeness at mean collateral $k=0.90$ with zero spurious deletions, plus an
auditable certificate that separates what deletion achieved from what it cannot
(\S\ref{sec:method},\,\S\ref{sec:res-planner});
\item a \emph{measured} parametric floor and its limit result: under the strongest
modeled adversary, $30/81$ facts cannot be certified erased even at zero residual
survival. Measured as a recovery rate over $8$ samples per fact,
the count is a Monte-Carlo estimate stable to $\pm1$--$2$ facts at the $n{=}8$-sample boundary
(\S\ref{sec:res-rho});
\item evidence of generality: the same probe/planner/certificate stack, run on
three memory architectures, exposes residual survival in all three through
different by-design mechanisms, including the agent-loop failure above that prior
audits bypass (\S\ref{sec:res-converge}).
\end{itemize}

We call the resulting framework \textsc{Redact}: \textbf{Re}coverability
\textbf{D}ecomposition and \textbf{A}uditable \textbf{C}o-Dele\textbf{t}ion.

The breadth across systems is evidence for a stronger, causally-decomposed notion
of erasure. This is not a claim to be first to evaluate these systems, since ForgetEval
\citep{forgeteval} already benchmarks all three, but a stronger question asked of
them. What is new is asking, and answering, why deleted facts return and what it
costs to make them stay gone.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Auditing forgetting in agent memory.} The closest prior work is
\textsc{ForgetEval} \citep{forgeteval}, the most thorough existing audit of
forgetting in deployed agent memory: a careful retrieval-based protocol spanning
Mem0, Graphiti, Letta, and roughly a dozen further systems. We build directly
on it. Its retrieval-based criterion is the \emph{necessary-but-not-sufficient}
gap of \S\ref{sec:intro}. Our
contribution is therefore the stronger, channel-decomposed criterion, not the
system coverage ForgetEval already establishes; correspondingly, our Mem0 figures
(recoverability by \emph{any} channel) are complementary to its top-$k$
forget-command rates, not in tension with them. Two of our results extend it
directly. ForgetEval deliberately bypasses Letta's agent loop (addressing
archival endpoints to keep the model out of the recall path), which is exactly the
loop we exercise and find surface-incomplete (\S\ref{sec:res-converge}); and its
own per-system observations (Graphiti shedding synthesized fact strings, Mem0's
router favoring link-and-keep) name the mechanisms our decomposition isolates,
independently corroborating the convergence result.

\paragraph{Unlearning methods.} Classical machine unlearning removes a training
example by retraining or editing model weights
\citep{bourtoule2021unlearning,cao2015towards,nguyen2022survey}. \textsc{Agentic
Unlearning} \citep{agenticunlearning} carries this to agents, separating parametric
from memory recovery (its ``backflow''), but still assumes \emph{weight access} to
edit the model; we operate black-box and memory-store-only, so we neither claim the
parametric/memory split as novel nor perform model surgery.

\paragraph{Deletion under dependencies.} Our formal grounding is the database line
on erasure when records are inter-derivable: P2E2's meaningful-erasure criterion
\citep{p2e2} and inference-aware deletion \citep{seqdb}. We port P2E2's
\emph{dependency-bounded} criterion to agent memory and deliberately
\emph{strengthen} it (its baseline keeps pre-existing operands present, which is
vacuous for the right to be forgotten) while inheriting its NP-hardness. This
bridge, from relational deletion theory to LLM agent memory, is one the
agent-memory works above do not make, and it is where the next section begins.

\section{Problem Formulation}\label{sec:formulation}

\paragraph{Setup.} Let $f$ be a target fact with value $v$ and let $\Sigma(v)$ be
its set of ground-truth surface forms. A memory store $S$ is a set of records,
each carrying text; write $\mathrm{str}(r)$ for the set of surface strings
appearing in record $r$. A deletion operation yields a surviving store
$S'\subseteq S$. The adversary
$\mathcal{A}$ has full read access to $S'$, black-box access to a base model $M$,
and general world knowledge $W$; a query $q_f$ asks for $v$. Write
$\mathrm{rec}(a)=1$ iff answer $a$ reveals a surface form in $\Sigma(v)$ (within
the stated value), as scored by the validated recovery judge
(\S\ref{sec:res-judge}). Certification is relative to a tolerance
$\tau\in(0,1)$ (we use $\tau{=}0.10$; \S\ref{sec:results}). Two of the three
channels below depend on $\mathcal{A}$'s reasoning model, so recoverability is
\emph{adversary-relative} (Def.~4).

\paragraph{Definition 1 (Residual survival).}
$\sigma_{\mathrm{res}}(f,S')=\mathds{1}\!\left[\exists\, r\in S':\;
\Sigma(v)\cap\mathrm{str}(r)\neq\varnothing\right]$: a surviving record physically
contains a surface form of $v$.

\paragraph{Definition 2 (Re-derivation).}
$\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}^{\mathcal{A}}(f,S')=\mathrm{rec}\!\left(\mathcal{A}(q_f \mid
S',M,W)\right)$: the adversary reconstructs $v$ by reasoning over the
\emph{surviving store} plus world knowledge.

\paragraph{Definition 3 (Parametric floor).}
$\rho^{\mathcal{A}}(f)=\mathrm{rec}\!\left(\mathcal{A}(q_f \mid \varnothing, M, C)\right)$, where
$C\subseteq W$ is the subject's world-knowable context and the store is
\emph{empty}. $\rho^{\mathcal{A}}$ has no dependence on $S$ and is therefore
\emph{system-independent}; we estimate it as a recovery rate over stochastic
samples.

\paragraph{The three channels, in words.} A deleted home address that survives
verbatim in a leftover summary note is \emph{residual survival}
($\sigma_{\mathrm{res}}$); a deleted salary an adversary rebuilds from a surviving
job title plus a public pay band is \emph{re-derivation} ($\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}$);
and a deleted ``holds a Class-3 licence,'' whose implication ``at least 18'' any
model already knows from world knowledge alone, is the \emph{parametric floor}
($\rho$). The first two shrink as one deletes more; the third does not.
Recoverability is the worst of the three, and $\rho$ is its irreducible lower bound.

\paragraph{Definition 4 (Recoverability, adversary-relative).}
$R_{\mathcal{A}}(f,S')=\max\!\left(\sigma_{\mathrm{res}},\,
\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}^{\mathcal{A}},\,\rho^{\mathcal{A}}\right)$; the superscript
marks the two adversary-dependent channels (residual is adversary-independent).
The $\max$ is the worst-case-channel principle: one open channel suffices, so a
rational adversary uses whichever is open. True recoverability is
$\sup_{\mathcal{A}}R_{\mathcal{A}}$; we bracket it with \textbf{four concrete
adversaries} spanning open and frontier models---$\mathcal{A}_{\text{mini}}$
(\texttt{gpt-4o-mini}), $\mathcal{A}_{4o}$ (\texttt{gpt-4o}), $\mathcal{A}_{\text{S5}}$
(Claude~Sonnet~5), and $\mathcal{A}_{\text{G5.5}}$ (GPT-5.5)---so the per-reasoner
values of $\rho$ are principled points bracketing the floor, not measurement noise.
A diverse panel matters because adversary strength is not one-dimensional: the
frontier models re-derive benign facts more readily yet \emph{refuse} sensitive
attribute inferences, so which adversary is worst varies by fact
(\S\ref{sec:res-rho}).

\paragraph{Definition 5 (Floor-reaching and erasure-certification).}
The \emph{never-stored} recoverability of $f$ is $\rho^{\mathcal{A}}(f)$: what
$\mathcal{A}$ recovers had $f$ never been stored, with \emph{every} stored operand
excluded. A deletion is \textbf{floor-reaching} for $\mathcal{A}$ iff
$\sigma_{\mathrm{res}}=\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}^{\mathcal{A}}=0$---recoverability is
driven to the parametric floor and nothing further is deletable---and
\textbf{erasure-certified} iff it is floor-reaching for every modeled adversary and
additionally $\sup_{\mathcal{A}} R_{\mathcal{A}}(f,S')=\max_{\mathcal{A}}
\rho^{\mathcal{A}}<\tau$, i.e.\ the floor is below $\tau$ under the worst modeled
adversary.

\paragraph{Stronger than P2E2 (and why).} This criterion is deliberately
\emph{stronger} than the dependency-bounded erasure of \citet{p2e2}, whose
baseline is the pre-insertion database with other, pre-existing facts
\emph{present}; under it, deleting $f$'s own records already suffices even when $f$
re-derives from surviving independent operands, so our operand co-deletion would
count as over-deletion. We reject that baseline as \emph{vacuous for the right to
be forgotten}: a diagnosis still re-inferable from stored symptoms has not been
forgotten. We therefore set the baseline at $\rho^{\mathcal{A}}$ (world knowledge
only, every stored operand removed) and cite P2E2 as the weaker criterion we
strengthen.

\paragraph{Remark 1 (the limit result, C5).} Floor-reaching is \emph{always}
achievable by co-deletion (remove operands until $\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}=0$);
erasure-\emph{certification} fails exactly when $\rho^{\mathcal{A}}\geq\tau$,
because $\rho$ has no store dependence and no co-deletion can lower it. The
certificate then reports \emph{floor-reaching but not erasure-certified} even at
$\sigma_{\mathrm{res}}=\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}=0$ (e.g.\ R11, Table~\ref{tab:certs}):
the fact is as erased as deletion can make it, yet remains world-inferable. This
split (always floor-reaching, certification gated by $\rho<\tau$) is exact and
avoids the misleading ``complete but recoverable.''

\paragraph{Remark 2 (residual--floor boundary, C1/C5).} The same constraining
context can land in either channel depending on \emph{where it lives}. If $v$ is
pinned by a stored operand $o\in S'$, the recovery is re-derivation
($\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}$) and is removable by co-deleting $o$; if $v$ is pinned by
world-knowable context $C\subseteq W$, the recovery is parametric ($\rho$) and is
\emph{not} removable. Our multi-hop operands are synthetic and live only in $S'$,
so their world-knowledge component is generic and inert without the stored
operand: $\rho=0$ and $\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}\!\to\!0$ under co-deletion
(\S\ref{sec:res-rederiv}). Our gradient contexts are themselves world-knowable and
target-constraining, so they enter $W$: $\rho>0$ (\S\ref{sec:res-rho}). This
boundary (deletable re-derivation vs.\ irreducible floor) is the C1/C5 spine.

\paragraph{Remark 3 (over-deletion is the measured price of erasure).}
Erasure-certification deletes entailers (e.g.\ C001, C002) the user never named,
which a reviewer may read as a data-minimization violation. It is the opposite:
co-deleting un-requested entailers is the \emph{measured, minimized} cost (the
collateral $k$) of an actual erasure guarantee, because leaving them is not
minimization but \emph{failed} erasure ($f$ simply re-derives). The planner
minimizes $k$ (\S\ref{sec:method}), the floor $\rho$ bounds what no deletion can
remove, and the certificate \emph{surfaces} $k$, so paying it (strong erasure)
versus not (data minimization) is an explicit, audited policy choice rather than a
hidden side effect.

\paragraph{The optimization problem (Opt-P2E2).} Let $R_f\subseteq S$ be $f$'s own
records. We seek the minimal-collateral co-deletion that makes $f$ floor-reaching:
\begin{equation*}
\min_{D\,\subseteq\, S\setminus R_f} |D| \quad\text{s.t.}\quad
\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}^{\mathcal{A}}\!\left(f,\; S\setminus(R_f\cup D)\right) = 0 ,
\end{equation*}
where $|D|=k$ is the surfaced collateral cost (Remark~3); erasure-certification
additionally requires $\rho^{\mathcal{A}}<\tau$ (Def.~5).

\paragraph{Proposition 1 (NP-hardness).} \textsc{Opt-P2E2} is NP-hard, by
reduction \emph{from} \textsc{Minimum Hitting Set}
\citep{karp1972reducibility,garey1979computers}. Given a universe $U$ and sets
$\mathcal{S}=\{S_1,\dots,S_m\}$ with $S_j\subseteq U$, construct an instance with
one fact $x_u$ per element $u\in U$ and, for each $S_j$, an entailment
$\bigwedge_{u\in S_j} x_u \vdash f$ (so each $S_j$ is a re-derivation path); set
$\sigma_{\mathrm{res}}=0$ and $\rho=0$. The surviving store re-derives $f$ iff some
path $S_j$ survives intact, so a co-deletion $D$ achieves
$\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}=0$ iff $D\cap S_j\neq\varnothing$ for every $j$---i.e.\
$\{u:x_u\in D\}$ is a hitting set of $\mathcal{S}$. Thus $\min|D|$ equals the
minimum hitting set, which is NP-hard. \textsc{Vertex Cover} is the $2$-uniform
special case, which is exactly our dataset's pairwise-entailment regime (e.g.\
F040 re-derived from C001$\wedge$C002): \emph{hardness already holds when every
fact has exactly two operands}, so the difficulty is intrinsic, not an artifact of
high-arity rules. P2E2's relational-deletion hardness \citep{p2e2} corroborates.
``A feasible $D$ hits every minimal re-implying subset'' is the intuition; the
hardness comes from the reduction. We therefore adopt the greedy,
entailment-confidence-ordered planner of \S\ref{sec:method}, exact on the small
dependency graphs seen in practice (mean $k=0.90$, \S\ref{sec:res-planner}).

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

Our pipeline instantiates each object of \S\ref{sec:formulation} as a concrete,
reusable component behind a single \texttt{MemorySystemAdapter} interface, so the
identical probe/planner/certificate stack runs unchanged on all three systems.

\paragraph{Probe battery.} Three probes measure the three channels of Def.~4.
The \emph{exact-match} probe scans every surviving record for a surface form of
$v$ ($\sigma_{\mathrm{res}}$). The \emph{re-derivation} probe gives the adversary
the full surviving store as notes (plus today's date and general world knowledge)
and elicits $v$ in a value-first format ($\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}^{\mathcal{A}}$).
The \emph{parametric} probe withholds the store entirely, conditioning only on the
subject's world-knowable context, and estimates $\rho^{\mathcal{A}}$ as a recovery
rate over $n$ stochastic samples. The re-derivation \emph{probe} reads the full
surviving store (the threat model) and is distinct from the operands-only
\emph{injection control} of \S\ref{sec:res-rederiv}, which instead never stores the
target value so as to isolate $\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}$ at $\sigma_{\mathrm{res}}=0$.
Recovery is decided by the validated judge
(\S\ref{sec:res-judge}): a substring match on the ground-truth surface forms,
falling back to an LLM adjudication for numeric/semantic approximation. Three
auxiliary probes support specific results: a \emph{paraphrase} probe (residual by
implication), a retrieval-score \emph{membership-inference} probe (\S\ref{sec:limitations}),
and a \emph{KG-summary} probe for the graph system (\S\ref{sec:res-converge}).

\paragraph{Entailment detector.} Re-derivation paths are found by an
LLM-as-judge that scores, for a candidate surviving fact (or set), the confidence
that it entails $v$ given world knowledge, returning a value in $[0,1]$
(\textsc{partial}/\textsc{no} are capped so a near-miss cannot read as full
entailment). The detector both draws the dependency edges and orders co-deletion.
Its validation (\S\ref{sec:res-judge}) dictates the choice of \texttt{gpt-4o}
here, since a judge that false-fires on partial operands inflates the collateral.

\paragraph{Co-deletion planner.} Against a \emph{naive} baseline that deletes only
the single surfaced record (no co-deletion; \S\ref{sec:res-residual}), we implement
two co-deletion heuristics. \emph{Threshold} (the method) probes, purges the
target's own artifacts using the narrow deletion surface form, then co-deletes
entailing facts in descending entailment confidence, re-probing after each until
$\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}^{\mathcal{A}}<\tau$, making $f$ floor-reaching with a
greedily minimal $D$. \emph{Depth-first} (an aggressive comparator) co-deletes
every entailer above $\tau$ in one shot, with no re-probe stopping rule. The
reported collateral $k=|D|$ is the measured price of the strong erasure notion
(Remark~3); the threshold heuristic minimizes it ($k=0.90$ vs.\ depth-first's
$5.65$, \S\ref{sec:res-planner}).

\paragraph{Certificate emitter.} For each target the emitter records the three
channel scores, the final recoverability $R_{\mathcal{A}}=\max(\cdot)$, the
heuristic, the co-deleted set and its size $k$, and the threat model. It exposes
\emph{two} machine-readable predicates from Def.~5: \texttt{floor\_reaching}
($\sigma_{\mathrm{res}}=\sigma_{\mathrm{red}}<\tau$, the deletable channels closed)
and \texttt{completeness\_certified}. The latter certifies erasure only under the
\emph{worst modeled adversary} (the $\sup$ of Def.~4, i.e.\
$\rho=\max_{\mathcal{A}}\rho^{\mathcal{A}}<\tau$), so a fact recoverable by the
stronger reasoner is reported uncertified even when the weaker one fails; this
conservative choice is why $30/81$, more than any single reasoner alone flags, are uncertifiable
(\S\ref{sec:res-rho}). A \texttt{floor\_reaching} but uncertified verdict is
precisely the limit result (Remark~1), now an audited field. The certificate thus
surfaces both the price ($k$) and the limit ($\rho$) of erasure; a recall caveat
(\S\ref{sec:res-judge}) qualifies certified verdicts, while uncertified ones are
sound lower bounds.

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:results}

We evaluate on three deployed agent-memory systems spanning three architecture
families: \textbf{Mem0} \citep{mem0} (\texttt{mem0ai} 2.0.7, a deduplicating
extraction pipeline over local Chroma), \textbf{Zep/Graphiti}
\citep{rasmussen2025zep} (\texttt{graphiti-core} 0.29.2, a bi-temporal knowledge
graph over Neo4j~5.26), and \textbf{Letta/MemGPT} \citep{packer2024memgpt}
(\texttt{letta}~0.16.8, an LLM-paging agent over Postgres + pgvector). Every
component runs locally with pinned versions and disabled telemetry, so the
pipeline is reproducible from a clean checkout. The deletion adversary has full
read access to the surviving store and black-box access to the base model (no
training-time access). Wherever recovery is model-dependent it is measured against
a four-reasoner adversary panel spanning open and frontier models: %
\texttt{gpt-4o-mini}, \texttt{gpt-4o}, Claude~Sonnet~5, and GPT-5.5. The
certificate takes the worst of them (Def.~4); embeddings are local
\texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2}. The completeness threshold is $\tau=0.10$. Datasets are
synthetic subjects placed in realistic statistical contexts: 84 isolated PII facts,
92 multi-hop facts (including a multi-level set whose targets are entailed
only through \emph{unstored} intermediate facts) with their stored operands and
bystanders, and 81 parametric-gradient facts across three difficulty tiers.
Recovery is scored by a validated LLM judge \citep{zheng2023judge} with no observed
false accepts (\S\ref{sec:res-judge}: $0/8$ gold negatives, $95\%$ Wilson upper
bound $\approx\!0.32$), so under that bound \emph{every reported recovery rate is a
conservative lower bound}. Table~\ref{tab:master} summarizes

\subsection{Minimal Co-Deletion: The Planner}\label{sec:res-planner}

On the full 92 multi-hop targets, the threshold planner
(probe~$\to$ purge the target's own artifacts~$\to$ co-delete entailing facts in
entailment-confidence order, re-probing until recoverability $<\tau$) achieves
\textbf{100\% completeness} (Wilson $[0.90,1.0]$) \textbf{with 0 spurious bystander
deletions} and a \textbf{mean collateral of $k=0.90$} (bootstrap $[0.82,0.99]$);
minimality spares 36 operands whose deletion was unnecessary once a sufficient
subset was removed.

\textbf{Control.} The 0-spurious figure \emph{is} the selectivity test: the
candidate pool per target is the fact's ground-truth operands plus four fixed
bystanders the planner must not touch (a small, controlled distractor set; a
full $34$-bystander pool also holds selectivity, $0$ spurious at $k{=}1.0$). The
result depends on the entailment judge, and we use the stronger one deliberately:
swapping in \texttt{gpt-4o-mini} inflates collateral (mean $k$ $0.90{\to}1.0$ and
$0{\to}2$ spurious bystander deletions on the same $34$ targets), because our
entailment-judge validation (\S\ref{sec:res-judge}) shows \texttt{gpt-4o-mini}
false-fires on $41.7\%$ of \emph{insufficient} partial operands while
\texttt{gpt-4o} false-fires on $0\%$. Using \texttt{gpt-4o} is precisely what
prevents $k$ from inflating. The
planner targets the NP-hard Opt-P2E2 problem; ``minimal'' here denotes a
sufficient co-deletion subset, not a certified optimum.

\textbf{Comparator.} Depth-first is a deliberately naive upper bound: one
probe-free pass that co-deletes every candidate scoring above $\tau$. Because a
correctly-judged ``NO'' is floored at $0.2>\tau$ (a load-bearing clamp that keeps
single multi-hop operands, which do not entail alone, in the pool), this $\tau$
filter is effectively inert, so depth-first co-deletes essentially the whole
candidate pool: it reaches $100\%$ completeness but at \textbf{$k=5.65$} with $126$
spurious bystander deletions (a probe-free pass cannot detect when to stop). Threshold attains the \emph{same} completeness at
\textbf{$k=0.90$}: iterative re-probing converts that guarantee into a
$\sim\!6\times$ reduction in collateral ($6.05\!\to\!0.90$) and full selectivity
($126\!\to\!0$ spurious). Naive single-record deletion (\S\ref{sec:res-residual})
is the lower baseline: one record, no re-derivation path closed.

\subsection{The Parametric Floor and the Limit of Certifiable Deletion}\label{sec:res-rho}

We \emph{measure} the parametric floor
$\rho$ (the rate at which the base model alone reconstructs a value from
world-knowable context) rather than asserting it, on $n{=}81$ facts authored
across a low/mid/high gradient. We report by the $\rho$ we \emph{measure}, not the
authored tier: the two disagree for $19/81$ facts, which we retain and report by
measured $\rho$ rather than discard: authored tiers are hypotheses, measured
$\rho$ is the evidence. The worst-adversary
$\rho_{\max}{=}\max_{\mathcal{A}}\rho^{\mathcal{A}}$ over our four modeled
adversaries (\texttt{gpt-4o-mini}, \texttt{gpt-4o}, Claude~Sonnet~5, and GPT-5.5)
splits the $n{=}81$ facts into \textbf{51 at $\rho_{\max}{\le}\tau$} (certifiable),
\textbf{18 at $\rho_{\max}{\ge}0.5$} (a hard floor), and \textbf{12 in the
intermediate band} ($\tau<\rho_{\max}<0.5$) (Table~\ref{tab:rho}). By authored tier
the worst-adversary means climb from \textbf{low $0.00$}, through \textbf{mid
$\sim\!0.1$}, to \textbf{high $\sim\!0.6$}. Certifying erasure under the \emph{worst}
modeled adversary (the $\sup$ of Def.~4), \textbf{30 of 81 certificates are
\emph{not} erasure-certifiable} at $\tau{=}0.1$ ($\rho_{\max}>\tau$) \emph{even
though residual survival is $0$}. The worst-adversary $\rho$ forms a \emph{measured
gradient} of parametric recoverability (from $51$ facts at the certifiable floor,
through $12$ in an intermediate band, to $18$ at a hard floor) rather than an
all-or-nothing split. Because the floor is graded, the uncertifiable count is
genuinely \emph{threshold-dependent}: $\tau$ is a deliberate \emph{policy dial}, not
a technicality, with real facts on either side of wherever an auditor sets it: %
choosing $\tau$ is choosing how much residual world-inferability to tolerate. This
is the limit result: when the value is recoverable from non-deletable world
knowledge, no co-deletion can certify erasure. The four-adversary panel exposes a
subtlety in \emph{who} the worst adversary is. On benign re-derivation the frontier
models leak \emph{more} than the open ones (\S\ref{sec:res-rederiv}); but on
\emph{sensitive} high-tier facts (a national-service status that implies sex, a
licence class that implies a minimum age) GPT-5.5 and Sonnet~5 \emph{refuse} (their
safety filters read attribute inference as disallowed; $20$ refusal flags, several
unanimous), so their measured $\rho$ there is a refusal-based \emph{lower} bound and
the worst-adversary high-tier $\rho$ is set by \texttt{gpt-4o}. Stronger models are
thus not \emph{uniformly} stronger adversaries: an adversary-relativity that taking
the $\sup$ over a diverse panel is designed to capture. Because $\rho$ is a recovery
rate over $8$ stochastic samples per fact with no reproducible server-side seed, the
count carries a Monte-Carlo sensitivity of about $\pm1$--$2$ facts at the
single-recovery boundary, leaving the limit result intact.
This gradient $\rho>0$ does not contradict the $\rho{=}0$ of the multi-hop facts
in \S\ref{sec:res-rederiv}: there the world-knowledge component is generic and
inert without the (fictional) stored operand, so co-deletion collapses $\rho$ to
$0$; here the context is \emph{itself} world-knowable \emph{and}
target-constraining, so $\rho$ stays positive. This residual--vs--floor boundary
is the C1/C5 spine and is formalized in \S\ref{sec:formulation}.

\textbf{Control.} The $\rho$ probe sees \emph{only} the subject's world-knowable
context, never the stored facts, so co-deletion provably cannot lower it. Three
hardening checks back the gradient: a mid-tier numeric-tolerance sweep shows the
floor persists at every tolerance (its magnitude, not its existence, is
tolerance-dependent); a base-rate vs.\ context-lift split separates genuine
inference from prior plausibility; and a high-tier refusal audit (e.g., the
sensitive-attribute item R13, refused on $4/8$ samples by \texttt{gpt-4o-mini})
means $\rho$ for sensitive attributes is an \emph{adversarial lower bound}. A
\textsc{complete} certificate is complete \emph{modulo} the recovery judge's
$\approx\!78\%$ recall; \textsc{incomplete} and aggregate figures are safe lower
bounds.

\subsection{Decomposing Recovery: Re-Derivation at Zero Residual}\label{sec:res-rederiv}

Using an operands-only control, the post-deletion state in
which the target's own value is never stored, so residual survival is $0$ by
construction (verified with the narrow deletion surface form, not the broad
recovery set, so an operand sharing a token is not miscounted), we isolate the
re-derivation channel. On Mem0, across our four-adversary panel
(\texttt{gpt-4o-mini}, \texttt{gpt-4o}, Sonnet~5, GPT-5.5) the leak is
\textbf{$94$--$100\%$ for stored-alone (bin~1) and $56/59/68/74\%$ for
stored+world (bin~2)} ($n{=}34$ per bin), collapsing to \textbf{$0\%$ after
co-deleting the operands} (Wilson $[0,.10]$), with parametric floor $\rho=0\%$.
Here the frontier models are the \emph{stronger} adversaries: bin~2 re-derivation
rises monotonically from $56\%$ (\texttt{gpt-4o-mini}) to $74\%$ (GPT-5.5): the
worst case the certificate must anticipate. A new multi-level set stresses
the planner's reach: each target is entailed not by stored facts but by
\emph{derived} intermediates, which are themselves entailed by stored roots
(a depth-2 join $A\!\wedge\!B\Rightarrow T$ and a depth-3 chain
$A\Rightarrow B\Rightarrow C\Rightarrow T$; $n{=}12$ each). All four adversaries
re-derive these at $100\%$, and co-deletion again drives recovery to $0\%$, but
only because the planner recurses \emph{past} the target's (unstored) direct
entailers to the stored roots, which a one-hop planner would miss. The same control
ports to Letta (exp11, four adversaries, $n{=}34$/bin): bin~1 $100\%$,
bin~2 $74$--$78\%$, again $\to 0\%$ after co-deletion, with faithful direct
co-deletion verified at $100\%$ and bystanders intact at $100\%$. F043 (the CHS$\to$Bishan
geographic leap) is a \emph{consistent} honest negative: it does not leak on
Mem0 \emph{or} Letta under either reasoner.
Re-derivation is thus a recovery channel orthogonal to residual survival, and
minimal co-deletion closes it down to the parametric floor.

\textbf{Control.} Recovery is binned by mechanism and \emph{never} aggregated
across bins, and run on four reasoners, because re-derivation is model-dependent
(the honest negative F043, a CHS$\to$Bishan geographic leap, does not leak on
either). On Letta the co-deletion is issued through the
\emph{direct, deterministic} \texttt{passages.delete} (by captured passage id),
not a conversational request, and we \emph{verify} every operand passage is gone:
faithful co-deletion $=100\%$, bystanders intact $=100\%$. ``Re-derivation
closed'' therefore cannot be confounded with ``the delete silently did not
execute'', keeping this result cleanly separate from the agent-mediated-deletion
finding of \S\ref{sec:res-converge}.

Residual survival is not a Mem0 quirk; it recurs across all three families by
\emph{different by-design mechanisms}. The two cross-system probes here are run on
$n{=}10$ sensitive-PII targets per system (a deliberately small, high-stakes set;
see \S\ref{sec:limitations}). In \textbf{Graphiti}, the explicit
\texttt{remove\_episode} is clean for the graph: it hard-deletes the episode,
its \texttt{RELATES\_TO} edges, and orphaned entities (we verify the hard delete
first). Yet the deleted fact survives in \textbf{stale entity and community
summaries that are not recomputed on deletion}: edge residue $40\%$ but
\textbf{KG/summary residue $80\%$} (residual counts a value present in any surviving
edge or summary regardless of its bi-temporal \texttt{invalid\_at}/\texttt{expired\_at},
consistent with the full-read-access threat model). In \textbf{Letta}, deletion is agent-mediated
across two surfaces (core blocks and archival passages). An explicit dual-surface
instruction is \textbf{$100\%$ faithful}; but a \emph{vague, deployment-realistic}
right-to-be-forgotten request (``I'm not comfortable with you keeping X'') with
the fact also in archival makes the agent scrub the core block it reasons about
and silently miss archival: \textbf{$0\%$ faithful, $100\%$ archival
residue, $0\%$ core residue} (Figure~\ref{fig:agentloop}). Agent-mediated deletion
is \emph{surface-incomplete} and instruction-dependent.

\textbf{Novelty and corroboration.} This agent-loop failure is genuinely new:
ForgetEval \citep{forgeteval} deliberately bypassed Letta's agent loop
(addressing the archival REST endpoints directly, keeping the LLM out of the
recall hot path), so we test exactly the path it excluded. Conversely,
ForgetEval's own per-system notes \emph{independently corroborate} our convergence
thesis: its observation that Graphiti ``sheds surface forms / synthesised fact
strings'' is our stale-summary residue, and that Mem0's router prefers
``link-and-keep over delete-old'' is our duplication residue.

\textbf{Control.} For Graphiti, verifying the clean hard delete \emph{first}
establishes that the residue is stale summaries, not failed edge removal. For
Letta, the $100\%$-faithful explicit baseline isolates the failure to
phrasing/surface coverage, not to a missing capability.

\subsection{Residual Survival and the Duplication Mechanism}\label{sec:res-residual}

Naive single-record deletion on Mem0 is residually
incomplete because the store \emph{silently duplicates} facts. Deleting the single
surfaced record leaves the value present in \textbf{$96.4\%$} of isolated PII
targets ($46/48$, Wilson $[86.0,98.9]$; exp01); an artifact-aware deletion drives
residual survival from \textbf{$96.4\%$ to $0\%$} (naive $[90.0,98.8]$, aware
$[0,7.4]$; exp02). Both arms run on the same $n{=}48$ isolated PII set whose probe
values are \emph{globally distinct} (a cross-fact uniqueness gate now enforces it),
so each fact's naive measurement is independent of the others'; exp01's $96.4\%$
and exp02's $96.4\%$ naive rates are two independent stochastic-dedup draws of the
same residual-incompleteness phenomenon, agreeing to within one or two facts.

\textbf{Control.} A $2{\times}2$ factorial over embedder
(MiniLM\,/\,OpenAI) $\times$ injection cadence ($0\,$s\,/\,$1.5\,$s) shows
value duplication in \emph{all four} cells, with paraphrase-variant duplicates
outnumbering byte-identical ones in every cell; over the facts whose value is
unique in the corpus (so vocabulary overlap cannot be miscounted as duplication),
the incidence is $76$--$78\%$. The duplication is therefore a semantic-dedup
\emph{design limitation}, not an artifact of our embedder or an injection-timing
race; it is independently documented in Mem0 issues \#4896 (hash-only dedup),
\#4573 ($\sim\!37.6\%$ near-duplicates in a production audit), and \#687 (the
byte-identical write race). We note that these rates are \emph{not} in tension
with ForgetEval's Mem0 numbers: their figure is forget-command success in
top-$k$ retrieval, whereas ours is post-deletion recoverability by \emph{any}
channel: complementary measurements of necessary vs.\ sufficient conditions for
completeness.

\subsection{Judge Validation}\label{sec:res-judge}

Two LLM judges are load-bearing, so both are validated against gold labels. The
\textbf{recovery judge} produced \textbf{no false accepts} on the gold set
($n{=}17$; 7 true positives, 0 false positives, 8 true negatives, 2 false
negatives; $\kappa=0.767$ vs.\ gold). Because this rests on only $8$ negatives,
the $0$ false-accept count carries a $95\%$ Wilson upper bound of $\approx\!0.32$;
the lower-bound framing holds \emph{up to that bound}, and its two errors are both
\emph{conservative} rejections (e.g., scoring ``$\approx$SGD~3{,}142'' as a
non-recovery of $3{,}200$). Tightening this bound with an expanded gold set of
adversarial near-miss \emph{recoveries} (``$\approx$3{,}190'' for $3{,}200$;
``AB+'' for ``AB negative'') is the single most valuable validation extension
(\S\ref{sec:limitations}). The \textbf{entailment judge} is validated on $146$ pairs
including $78$ near-miss negatives (partial operands that should \emph{not}
entail): inter-judge agreement falls from $\kappa=0.83$ on trivial pairs to
$0.455$ on the $12$ curated near-misses, where \texttt{gpt-4o-mini} false-fires on
$41.7\%$ of insufficient operands versus $0\%$ for \texttt{gpt-4o}. Broadening the
test to all $66$ single operands across the multi-hop corpus (a fuzzier boundary,
since a lone operand can be near-sufficient) raises both rates ($72.7\%$ vs.\ $50\%$),
with \texttt{gpt-4o} still the more conservative. This is the direct
justification for using \texttt{gpt-4o} as the planner's entailment judge
(\S\ref{sec:res-planner}).

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}

We state the boundaries of the evidence, leading with issues we found and
corrected: the measurement discipline is itself part of what the paper offers.

\paragraph{A retracted, then powered, membership claim.} An early result
suggested a membership signal survived artifact-aware deletion; at $n{=}6$ it was
underpowered and we retracted it. Powered to $n{=}48$ members with \emph{two}
matched near-twins each ($96$ controls), bootstrap $95\%$ CIs, and a
label-permutation test, the picture is sharp: artifact-aware deletion restores
membership-indistinguishability (AUC $0.51$, CI $[0.42,0.62]$ includes $0.5$,
$p{=}.39$), the intact store is detectable (AUC $0.68$, $p{=}.001$, so the test
\emph{has} power), and, unlike the earlier underpowered $n{=}33$ run where it was
borderline ($p{=}.065$), the \textbf{naive-deletion signal is now significant}
(AUC $0.65$, CI $[0.56,0.74]$ excludes $0.5$, $p{=}.002$). At this power, naive
single-record deletion \emph{does} leave a detectable membership signal; only
artifact-aware deletion removes it.

\paragraph{Judge-dependent measurements, bounded.} Both LLM judges are validated,
neither is perfect. The entailment judge's inter-rater agreement falls to
$\kappa{=}0.455$ on hard near-miss negatives, where \texttt{gpt-4o-mini}
false-fires on $41.7\%$; we use \texttt{gpt-4o} (no false-fires on our cases) as
the planner's judge, but adversarial partial-operand entailments remain the
hardest case. The recovery judge had \emph{no} false accepts, but over only $8$
gold negatives (a $95\%$ Wilson upper bound of $\approx\!0.32$), so the
lower-bound framing holds up to that bound; tightening it with adversarial
near-miss \emph{recoveries} (e.g.\ ``$\approx$3{,}190'' for $3{,}200$) is the most
valuable validation extension. \textsc{Complete} verdicts are also modulo
the recovery judge's recall.

\paragraph{$\rho$ is a lower bound where it matters most.} The parametric floor is
estimated by eliciting the value from the base model; for sensitive attributes the
model often refuses (7 high-tier items drew refusals), so
the measured $\rho$ \emph{understates} true world-inferability for exactly the
attributes a privacy audit cares about. A more willing \emph{reasoner} could only push
the uncertifiable count above $30/81$, never below; this monotonicity is over reasoner
adversaries: the recovery judge is the single validated \texttt{gpt-4o-mini}.

\paragraph{The entailment graph must be known.} The decomposition and planner
assume the entailment graph is specified: the operands that re-imply a fact are
known, as they are in our controlled setup because we injected them. In a deployed
store the graph is neither given nor easily recovered: the planner closes only
re-derivation paths through \emph{known} entailers, so an unmodeled entailer is an
uncloseable path, and discovering the entailment graph of a live memory is itself
an open problem. This bounds the method's guarantee independently of the parametric
floor: even at $\rho{=}0$, completeness is relative to the entailers the auditor
can see.

\paragraph{Small cross-system samples.} The convergence result probes $n{=}10$
high-stakes targets per system (scaled up from an initial $n{=}3$). The mechanisms
are qualitatively distinct and each is independently corroborated
\citep{forgeteval}, but the per-system rates (e.g.\ $80\%$ KG/summary residue in
the graph store) remain indicative, not precise at this scale; still more targets
per family is the obvious next step. Finally, all subjects are synthetic,
so recoveries reflect re-derivability rather than memorized real individuals, and
we model two reasoner adversaries rather than the supremum over all of them.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}\label{sec:conclusion}

Deleting a record is not erasing a fact. We gave a causal decomposition of
post-deletion recoverability, a planner that closes the deletable channels with
minimal collateral, and a certificate that states, per fact, both the cost of
erasure and whether it can be certified at all. Across three memory architectures
the same gap recurs through different by-design mechanisms, and a \emph{measured}
parametric floor sets a hard limit on what any deletion can promise.

That limit is the paper's broadest implication. For a fact recoverable from world
knowledge alone ($\rho\geq\tau$), $30$ of our $81$ gradient facts under the
strongest modeled adversary, \emph{no} external-store deletion method can achieve
erasure, because the value can be reconstructed without ever reading the store.
For such facts, right-to-be-forgotten compliance cannot be a deletion operation:
it requires not storing the fact in the first place, or explicitly accepting and
disclosing the residual floor. Deletion engineering can close the re-derivation and
residual channels; it cannot close the parametric one.

One extension is immediate: adding a fourth memory
architecture to test whether the three-family convergence is universal. The deeper
open problem is certifying erasure against an unbounded adversary rather than a
modeled bracket: the floor we measure is a lower bound, so the true limit of
forgetting in agent memory may be higher still.
